Why patch at all?

Each voice already makes sound on its own — internal normalled connections wire it up like a hardwired synth. Running a cable from a jack overrides that default wiring and re-routes the signal by hand. That is the whole game: send an output somewhere new and the rack becomes a different instrument.

Wire on the Patchbay, play on the voices. Cables are drawn and removed on the PATCHBAY tab only — but the connection stays live everywhere, so you patch there and play on the CASCADE / ANVIL / MONARCH tabs.

Patchbay basics

  • Outputs feed inputs. Drag a cable from a bright-ring output to a dark-ring input. Two outputs can’t connect; one output can fan out to many inputs.
  • Four kinds of signal:
    • Audio — the actual sound (oscillators, filter, the VCA output you hear).
    • CV (control voltage) — a steering signal: pitch, filter cutoff, levels. Here CV is audio-rate, so you can even use an oscillator as a CV source for wild effects.
    • Gate — an on/off pulse meaning “note on / note off.”
    • Clock — a steady tick that keeps sequencers in time.
    Anything plugs into anything, but like-into-like (CV→CV, gate→gate) is the musical starting point.
  • Normalled jacks (dashed ring). A dashed ring means the input already has a built-in source. Example: Monarch ▸ GATE is normalled to its keyboard, so the keys play it by default — patch a cable into GATE and your cable takes over.
  • Cable budget. Up to 12 cables at once (the counter sits top-right of the patchbay). Click a cable’s curve to remove it; INIT (top ribbon) clears them all.

The three voices at a glance

CASCADE — polyrhythmic drone machine

Two oscillators, each with two sub-oscillators, two 4-step sequencers, and four rhythm generators. Built for evolving chords, drones, and cross-rhythms.

Outs: VCO 1/2 · VCO 1/2 SUB 1/2 · SEQ 1/2 (pitch) · SEQ 1/2 CLK · CLOCK
Ins: CUTOFF · VCO 1/2 · VCO/SUB/PWM · RHYTHM 1–4 · PLAY · RESET

ANVIL — the percussion brain

Two oscillators with FM and hard sync, plus an 8-step sequencer that sends both pitch and velocity per step. Drums, zaps, and gnarly basslines.

Outs: VCA (its sound) · PITCH · VELOCITY · TRIGGER · VCA/VCF/VCO EG · VCO 1/2
Ins: VCO 1/2 CV · 1→2 FM AMT · VCF/VCA/VCO DECAY · TRIGGER · VELOCITY

MONARCH — the classic mono synth

One oscillator (saw + pulse), a fat ladder filter, an LFO, a 32-step sequencer, and the keyboard. The go-to for basslines and leads.

Outs: VCA · VCF · VCO SAW/PULSE · LFO TRI/SQ · EG · KB · GATE · MULT 1/2
Ins: VCO 1V/OCT (pitch) · VCO LIN FM · VCF CUTOFF/RES · VCO MOD · LFO RATE · GATE

SAMPLER

Eight pads, each with an audio OUT and a TRIG input — fire any pad from any gate or clock in the rack.

Recipes

Each one is a small patch. Power on, switch to the PATCHBAY tab, drag the cables, then play on the voice tabs.

1Let the drum brain play the bass

Anvil ▸ PITCH→Monarch ▸ VCO 1V/OCT
Anvil ▸ TRIGGER→Monarch ▸ GATE

Why: Anvil’s 8-step sequencer sends a pitch and a trigger per step; routed into Monarch’s oscillator pitch and gate, the percussion sequencer now plays a melodic bassline on the Monarch.

Try: Dial the Anvil step PITCH knobs for the line, and shape the Monarch filter for the bass tone.

2Lock the whole rack to one clock

Cascade ▸ CLOCK→Anvil ▸ ADV / CLOCK
Cascade ▸ SEQ 1 CLK→Sampler ▸ TRIG 1

Why: One clock source advances Anvil’s sequencer and fires a sample, so the polyrhythms, the drum steps, and the samples all stay locked together.

Try: No cable needed for tempo alone — the TEMPO LINK button in the top ribbon slaves every voice’s tempo at once.

3Bell & metal tones (audio into the filter)

Monarch ▸ VCO SAW→Monarch ▸ VCF CUTOFF

Why: Feeding an audio-rate signal into the filter’s cutoff makes it scream and ring with inharmonic, bell-like overtones — the cutoff is being modulated thousands of times a second.

Try: Crank VCF RES, then play with the oscillator pitch. Swap in VCO PULSE, or a different voice’s oscillator, for a new flavour.

4Cross-FM clang

Cascade ▸ VCO 1→Monarch ▸ VCO LIN FM

Why: One oscillator frequency-modulates another, giving glassy, clangorous, DX-style metallic tones instead of a plain waveform.

Try: Sweep Cascade’s VCO 1 FREQ for the timbre, and detune the two oscillators for movement.

5One LFO, two filters

Monarch ▸ LFO TRI→Cascade ▸ CUTOFF

Why: Monarch’s LFO now wobbles the Cascade filter as well as its own, so both voices breathe with the same motion — coherent, hands-free movement.

Try: Slow the LFO RATE right down for a long, drifting sweep; speed it up for a tremolo/vibrato shimmer.

6Trigger samples from a sequencer

Anvil ▸ TRIGGER→Sampler ▸ TRIG 1
Cascade ▸ SEQ 2 CLK→Sampler ▸ TRIG 2

Why: Your drum samples fire in lockstep with the analog sequencers — layer a sampled kick under Anvil’s synth drum, and a hat on Cascade’s rhythm.

Try: On the SAMPLER tab, load different sounds onto the pads; each TRIG fires whatever pad it’s wired to.

7Make the bass duck to the drums

Anvil ▸ VCA EG→Monarch ▸ VCA CV

Why: Anvil’s percussive envelope now controls the Monarch’s volume, so the bass pumps and pulses in time with the drum hits — a built-in sidechain feel.

Try: Shorten the Anvil VCA DECAY for a tight pump; lengthen it for a slow swell.

8Drone through the ladder filter

Cascade ▸ VCO 1 SUB 1→Monarch ▸ EXT AUDIO

Why: Cascade’s deep sub-oscillator is sent through Monarch’s ladder filter (listen at the Monarch VCF / VCA output) — thick, processed drones from one voice run through another’s character.

Try: Patch Monarch’s LFO into its own VCF CUTOFF for slow filter motion on the drone.

Handy to know

  • Record what you play. On the MONARCH tab, arm REC on the step strip, then play the keyboard — each note is written to the pattern and the cursor advances, so you never have to dial in steps one at a time.
  • Master FX. The FX tab adds flanger, delay, and reverb across the whole mix — they sit on the master output, after everything.
  • Save your patch. SAVE (top ribbon) stores the whole rig — cables, knobs, sequences, effects — to a slot or a shareable file; PRESETS loads them back.

Glossary

The abbreviations on the panels and jacks, in plain English.

Sound sources & shaping

VCOVoltage-Controlled Oscillator
The part that makes the raw tone. Its pitch is set by a control voltage — that’s what a sequencer or keyboard steers.
SUBSub-oscillator
An extra oscillator tuned an octave (or two) below the main one, for added weight and bass.
NOISENoise generator
Random hiss. Great for percussion, wind, and texture; often mixed in or sent to the filter.
VCFVoltage-Controlled Filter
Removes frequencies to shape the brightness/tone. The “ladder” filter is the classic warm, round one.
VCAVoltage-Controlled Amplifier
Controls volume over time. An envelope drives it so each note swells and fades instead of just switching on.
EGEnvelope Generator
A shape over time — attack, decay, sustain — fired when a note hits. Usually moves volume or filter cutoff.
LFOLow-Frequency Oscillator
A slow oscillator, below hearing, used to wobble things: vibrato (pitch), tremolo (volume), or a filter sweep.

Waveforms & filter

SAW / PULSE / TRI / SQ
Oscillator waveforms — Sawtooth, Pulse, Triangle, Square — from bright and buzzy (saw) to hollow (pulse/square) to mellow (triangle).
CUTOFFfilter cutoff frequency
Where the filter starts cutting. Sweeping it is the classic “wah” / opening-up sound.
RESResonance
A boost right at the cutoff. Crank it and the filter whistles or screams; high enough and it self-oscillates into a tone.
HP / LPHigh-Pass / Low-Pass
Filter modes. LP keeps the lows (warms, darkens); HP keeps the highs (thins, brightens).
PW / PWMPulse Width / Pulse-Width Modulation
The shape of a pulse wave. Modulating it (PWM) gives a rich, shimmering, animated tone.

Modulation & control

CVControl Voltage
A steering signal you don’t hear — it sets pitch, cutoff, levels. Here CV is audio-rate, so it can also do wild, fast effects.
MODModulation
Using one signal to change another — e.g. an LFO modulating pitch for vibrato.
FM / LIN FMFrequency Modulation / Linear FM
Using one oscillator to rapidly modulate another’s pitch, for glassy, metallic, bell-like tones.
1V/OCTone volt per octave
The standard pitch-CV scaling — one step up raises pitch an octave. Patch a pitch CV here to play a voice.
MULTMultiple
A passive splitter: one signal in, the same signal out of several jacks, to send it to two or three places at once.
ASSIGNassignable output
A CV output you can route to a chosen destination — a spare modulation source.
VC MIXVoltage-Controlled Mixer
Blends or crossfades two signals, with the blend itself controllable by CV.

Sequencing & timing

SEQSequencer
Plays a repeating pattern of steps (pitches and gates) on its own.
GATE
An on/off pulse meaning “note on / note off” — it tells the envelope when to fire and release.
TRIGTrigger
A short pulse that fires a single event: a drum hit, a step, a sample.
CLKClock
A steady tick that keeps sequencers in time; patch one clock to many places to lock everything together.
ADVAdvance
Steps the sequencer forward one step — usually driven by a clock.
VELVelocity
How hard a note hits; here it can scale loudness or punch per step.
GLIDEportamento
Sliding smoothly in pitch from one note to the next instead of jumping.
ACCENT
An emphasis — louder/brighter — on a chosen sequencer step.
RATCHET
Repeating a step’s hit several times within the one step — a roll or stutter.
TIE
Holding a note across into the next step instead of re-triggering it.
REST
A silent step — no note plays.
BPMbeats per minute
The tempo — higher is faster.
TEMPO LINK
Slaves all the voices to one shared tempo so they stay in sync (top ribbon, no cable needed).
POLYRHYTHM
Running two or more rhythms of different lengths at once, so they drift in and out of phase — the heart of the Cascade.

Tuning & general

ET / JIEqual Temperament / Just Intonation
Two ways to tune the scale. ET spaces the steps evenly (like a piano); JI uses pure whole-number ratios (more consonant). The numbers (12, 8) are how many steps per octave.
OCTOctave
A doubling of pitch — the same note higher or lower.
KBKeyboard
The on-screen keys (and any connected MIDI keyboard), which play the Monarch voice.
EXTExternal (audio in)
An input for an outside sound to run through the voice’s filter and amplifier.
HARD SYNC
Forcing one oscillator to restart in step with another, for aggressive, cutting, tearing tones.
MIDI
A standard for connecting musical gear and controllers; here, plugging in a MIDI keyboard to play.
INITInitialize
Reset everything — knobs, cables, sequences — back to factory defaults (top ribbon).

Happy patching — there are no wrong cables, only new sounds.
